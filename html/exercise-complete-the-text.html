In total, there are @@twelve@@ notes, but when composing music we can select some of these notes and form @@scales@@. For example, if we choose only five notes, we obtain a @@pentatonic@@ scale. All scales start on the first note, which is called the @@tonic@@.

@@Major@@ scales have the following distances between notes: @@tone@@ – tone – @@semitone@@ – tone – @@tone@@ – tone – @@semitone@@.

From a major scale, we can obtain its relative minor scale starting from the @@sixth@@ note of the major scale. For example, in the do (C) major scale, its sixth note is @@la@@, so the relative @@minor@@ scale is la (A) @@minor@@. The distances between notes in a minor scale are: @@tone@@ – semitone – @@tone@@ – tone – @@semitone@@ – tone – @@tone@@.

We must not confuse the relative scale with the @@parallel@@ scale. For example, the parallel scale of do (C) major is do (C) @@minor@@.
